Interests

For any organization, thriving in an “adapt-or-die” world that changes at the relentless pace of technology is a continual challenge. For experience design firm, Method, it also presents a tremendous opportunity to build loyalty and adapt over time. With over 15 years of helping clients build better end-to-end customer experiences at Method, Chief Strategy Officer, Patrick Newbery, and CEO, Kevin Farnham, argue that experience design is the key building block for businesses that want to grow and innovate.

In their new book, Experience Design: A Framework for Integrating Brand, Experience, and Value, Newbery and Farnham build a common ground of understanding for design and business to help approach and solve these urgent and ongoing business challenges.

Experience design is based on using the concept behind the brand as a way to identify and define value for customers. And this becomes the purpose and intent of the business—to deliver products, services, and experiences that deliver the value that the brand represents, and reinforces the unique brand meaning. But it also means measuring that value from the customer’s perspective, and continually investigating new areas of value that are natural extensions for the brand.

Experience design provides a way for the business and the designer to both discuss objectives and options. It creates a way for business to invite design to the table earlier, and understand how design can help solve problems. And it also helps businesses rethink how they engage design partners in ways that are more likely to produce success with less risk.

In a robust and thought-provoking analysis, Newbery and Farnham provide business leaders with insight into ways to integrate brand purpose into products and service value and across the entire journey a customer has with a business. The goal is to build a strategic approach for helps business engage their customers through experiences that deliver value and strengthen the relationship between business and customer.

What excites me:

I love old tools, tables, notebooks. Things that have been the aid of countless hours of work or passion projects. Things that have see a ton of wear and tear. Something that has lasted long past it's manufactures expected lifespan. Something that you can see how it was held by the polish of where hands used it over and over. I love things that are seemingly simple, tried and true. They aren't a fad or the next thing. They aren't a multi-tool. They were designed with a singular purpose. No, they don't do everything; but the one thing they do, they do exceptionally well.

What I'm looking for:

I'm looking for or would love to assist in the making of a top to bottom typographic and visual as well as layout framework that can handle any amounts of data organization. A framework that doesn't just style and resize based on the container it's put in, but something that can look at the scope of the information and figure out how to best organize and optimize it to be the optimal set up for what it is and the content isn't dicatating design or the experience. That isn't to say that certain types of currated content shouldn't have an extra coat of polish added to differenciate it and make it feel special in some way.

Unpoppular opinion:

I don't think design should carry emotion. Yeah, design is there to serve a purpose or meet a goal. Otherwise it's art and should be handled much different. Emotions change and so can designs, but they shouldn't have that layer there if it isn't in service to the needs of the design or the client and project.

Design Philosophy

My design philosophy is one based around Maslow's Hierarchy of needs except instead of the needs of a singular person it is a project or goal. Every design is in service to a goal. Whether that is to better organize things or to sell something, the idea is the same and should be handled on the top level the same. Once there starts to be all this flash and sparkle added to it, it is only a matter of thime beforte it misses its mark and is being rediesigned again and costing more time and money when it could of been planned for in the preliminary stages.

The overall look of soemthing has a base level DNA (or the origins) of where it originated but it is then up to how a specifici addaptation or execution will use what is being made. I feel like the Flat Design trend has been of the best rends for design since desktop publishing firs tmade it's way onto the consumer computers. It has everything baked down to it's essense and level the content center stage for the user and clients to use and decide what it's value is.

It was an eye openbing experience to have because up to that point I had had everything planned and set in front of me that was to be laid out and then it was up to me to orgaizxz it the best way I could.

Working like this wasn entirely new and totlally out side of the norm for me. THe thing was though I had gotten into reading a ton of design philospohy and design systems for infographics. So I was doing the design planning research I didn't know that I needed. WIth infographics you still have all of the data upfront but the layout and designs are dictated by the content and to have a few recipes for succes in how to best organize it.
